\section{Verification}
\label{sec:verification}

\subsection{Taylor remainder test}
\label{sec:taylor_test}
The correctness of the adjoint gradient --- including exact chain-rule
propagation through the Helmholtz filter and Heaviside projection --- is
verified by a Taylor remainder test on the reduced functional $J(\rho)$.
For a random unit-norm perturbation $\delta\rho$, the first-order Taylor
remainder is
\begin{equation}
    R(\varepsilon) = \left|
        J(\rho + \varepsilon\,\delta\rho)
        - J(\rho)
        - \varepsilon\,\frac{\mathrm{d}J}{\mathrm{d}\rho}[\delta\rho]
    \right|.
\end{equation}
If the gradient is correct, $R(\varepsilon) = \mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^2)$;
a slope of~1 in $\log R$ vs.\ $\log\varepsilon$ would indicate a
gradient error.

The test is implemented in \texttt{micrograd/taylor\_test.py} and
executed automatically in the CI pipeline.
On the $20\times5$ mesh (for speed), the least-squares slope of
$\log_{10} R(\varepsilon)$ over $\varepsilon \in [10^{-8}, 10^{-4}]$
is $2.01 \pm 0.03$, confirming second-order convergence and correct
gradient computation.

\subsection{SUPG stabilisation validation}
Without stabilisation at the nominal operating conditions
($\Pe_h \approx 780$ on the $80\times20$ mesh at peak velocity),
the concentration field exhibits node-to-node oscillations with amplitude
up to~$0.4$.
SUPG reduces oscillation amplitude below $10^{-3}$ while changing the
outlet gradient slope by less than~$2\%$ relative to a reference
solution on a $160\times40$ mesh.
Full upwinding over-diffuses the profile, reducing the outlet slope by
approximately~$30\%$.
SUPG is therefore the preferred choice; full results are in
Supplementary~S3.

\section{Proof-of-concept: linear gradient generator}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Optimised design}
The framework was applied to the inverse design of a two-inlet microfluidic
concentration gradient generator with prescribed linear outlet profile
$c_{\mathrm{target}}(y) = y/L_y$ on a $2000\times500\,\mu$m domain.
After 400 iterations on the primary $80\times20$ mesh, the optimised design
achieves an outlet RMSE of $\rmseTopOpt$
($\rmseTopOptPP$\,pp of the $[0,1]$ range; Eq.~\eqref{eq:rmse})
and a hydraulic resistance of $\hydResTopOpt$\,Pa$\cdot$s/m$^2$
(per unit depth; Section~\ref{sec:dimensional}).
These results are obtained in approximately twenty minutes on a standard laptop.

\subsection{Comparison with the Christmas-tree baseline}
\label{sec:comparison}

The classic Christmas-tree network of Jeon~\emph{et~al.}~\cite{jeon2000}
was simulated on the same Brinkman mesh as a performance baseline.
Because the Christmas-tree topology is structurally constrained to
produce binomial concentration profiles and cannot be optimised for an
arbitrary target, this comparison demonstrates a \emph{capability gap}
rather than a marginal performance difference on the same objective.

Table~\ref{tab:metrics} summarises key performance metrics.
The topology-optimised design simultaneously reduces hydraulic resistance
by $\reduction\%$ (from $\treeHydRes$ to $\hydResTopOpt$\,Pa$\cdot$s/m$^2$)
and produces a substantially closer approximation to the linear target
(RMSE reduced from $\treeRMSE$ to $\rmseTopOpt$, a $72\%$ improvement
in outlet fidelity).
