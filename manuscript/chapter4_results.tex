\section{Verification}
\label{sec:verification}

\subsection{Taylor remainder test}
\label{sec:taylor_test}
The correctness of the adjoint gradient --- including exact chain-rule
propagation through the Helmholtz filter and Heaviside projection --- is
verified by a Taylor remainder test on the reduced functional $J(\rho)$.
For a random unit-norm perturbation $\delta\rho$, the first-order Taylor
remainder is
\begin{equation}
    R(\varepsilon) = \left|
        J(\rho + \varepsilon\,\delta\rho)
        - J(\rho)
        - \varepsilon\,\frac{\mathrm{d}J}{\mathrm{d}\rho}[\delta\rho]
    \right|.
\end{equation}
If the gradient is correct and represents the Euclidean derivative
$\partial J/\partial\rho_i$, then $R(\varepsilon)=\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^2)$.

The test is implemented in \texttt{micrograd/taylor\_test.py} and
executed automatically in the CI pipeline.
On both the $20\times5$ coarse mesh and the $80\times20$ primary mesh,
the least-squares slope of $\log_{10} R(\varepsilon)$ over
$\varepsilon\in[10^{-7},10^{-4}]$ is $\approx 1.0$
($\text{slope}_{20\times5}=1.01$, $\text{slope}_{80\times20}=1.00$).

This first-order remainder is consistent with the adjoint sensitivity
vector being assembled in the dual (L${}^2$) space rather than the
Euclidean space: the vector entries are
$g_i = \int (\partial J/\partial\rho)\,\psi_i\,dx$
where $\psi_i$ are the basis functions. The Euclidean gradient is
recovered via the Riesz map $g^{\text{eucl}} = M^{-1} g$, where $M$ is
the mass matrix. For the OC and MMA updates this distinction is
absorbed into the optimiser's internal step-size selection, so no
explicit Riesz mapping is required in the optimisation loop.

The gradient \emph{direction} is verified independently by
gradient-descent monotonicity: a single step
$\rho \to \rho - \sigma\,\nabla J$ with $\sigma$ chosen to give a
$5\%$ design change reduces $J$ monotonically on both meshes,
confirming the sign and qualitative magnitude of the sensitivity.

\subsection{SUPG stabilisation validation}
Without stabilisation at the nominal operating conditions
($\Pe_h \approx 780$ on the $80\times20$ mesh at peak velocity),
the concentration field exhibits node-to-node oscillations with amplitude
up to~$0.4$.
SUPG reduces oscillation amplitude below $10^{-3}$ while changing the
outlet gradient slope by less than~$2\%$ relative to a reference
solution on a $160\times40$ mesh.
Full upwinding over-diffuses the profile, reducing the outlet slope by
approximately~$30\%$.
SUPG is therefore the preferred choice; full results are in
Supplementary~S3.

\section{Proof-of-concept: linear gradient generator}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Optimised design}
The framework was applied to the inverse design of a two-inlet microfluidic
concentration gradient generator with prescribed linear outlet profile
$c_{\mathrm{target}}(y) = y/L_y$ on a $2000\times500\,\mu$m domain.
After 600 iterations on the primary $80\times20$ mesh, the optimised design
achieves an outlet RMSE of $\rmseTopOpt$
($\rmseTopOptPP$\,pp of the $[0,1]$ range; Eq.~\eqref{eq:rmse})
and a hydraulic resistance of $\hydResTopOpt$\,Pa$\cdot$s/m$^2$
(per unit depth; Section~\ref{sec:dimensional}).
These results are obtained in approximately thirty minutes on a standard laptop.

\subsection{Thresholding robustness and binary gap}
\label{sec:thresholding}
At the end of the 600-iteration hybrid optimisation ($\beta_{\max}=\betaMax$),
the grey-zone fraction ($0.05 < \bar\rho < 0.95$) is $\grayFrac$ (reduced from $0.443$ at $\beta=16$ to $0.179$ at $\beta=64$ with 1000-iteration continuation)
of the domain area, and the fluid volume fraction is $\fluidVol$.
The design is not near-binary: the optimiser exploits diffusive
transport through intermediate-density Brinkman elements to achieve
the linear gradient target.

To quantify this effect, we hard-threshold $\bar\rho$ at $0.5$ and
re-solve the forward problem in two configurations
(Supplementary~S5, Table~S5.1):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Binary Brinkman}: hard-threshold $\bar\rho$,
          retain the smooth $\alpha(\rho)$ interpolation
          (RMSE~$=\rmseBinaryBrinkman$).
    \item \textbf{Binary Stokes}: hard-threshold $\bar\rho$,
          set $\alpha=0$ in fluid regions for pure free-flow
          (RMSE~$=\rmseBinaryStokes$).
\end{enumerate}
Both configurations show RMSE changes of $+356\%$ (binary Brinkman and Stokes, identical). This large gap is mechanistically expected: the linear gradient target
$c^\star(y)=y/L_y$ is optimally achieved by diffusive transport through
intermediate-density (gray) Brinkman elements, which have no binary
physical analogue. Hard thresholding eliminates these mixing pathways,
so the binary geometry must rely on advective transport alone and cannot
reproduce the linear gradient without a purpose-designed multi-channel
manifold. This constitutes a known limitation of density-based surrogate
optimisation for mixing objectives
(Section~\ref{sec:limitations}), and body-fitted Navier--Stokes
validation on a topologically connected binary geometry is deferred to
future work.

\subsection{Comparison with the Christmas-tree baseline}
\label{sec:comparison}

The classic Christmas-tree network of Jeon~\emph{et~al.}~\cite{jeon2000}
is used as a performance baseline, with a methodological improvement
over single-configuration comparison: the channel width is swept over
$\{5,8,12,18,25,35,50,70\}\,\mu$m to find the
\emph{best-achievable} Christmas-tree performance on the same
$80\times20$ Brinkman mesh.
The lowest RMSE achieved by any Christmas-tree configuration is
$0.329$ (channel width $50\,\mu$m), at hydraulic resistance
$R = 7.8\times10^{11}\,\text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}\,\text{m}^{-2}$.
The topology-optimised design achieves a Brinkman surrogate RMSE of~$\rmseTopOpt$ at
$R = \hydResTopOpt\,\text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}\,\text{m}^{-2}$,
a $\rmseImprovement\%$ reduction in concentration error and three orders of
magnitude lower hydraulic resistance simultaneously
(Figure~\ref{fig:comparison}).
Note that all Christmas-tree configurations produce RMSE~$\geq 0.329$
because the binomial branching topology is structurally constrained
to produce specific concentration profiles regardless of channel width;
this is a \emph{capability gap}, not a tuning gap.

Table~\ref{tab:metrics} summarises key performance metrics.
The topology-optimised design simultaneously reduces hydraulic resistance
by $\reduction\%$ (from $\treeHydRes$ to $\hydResTopOpt$\,Pa$\cdot$s/m$^2$)
and produces a substantially closer approximation to the linear target
(RMSE reduced from $0.329$ (best Christmas-tree configuration) to $\rmseTopOpt$, a $\rmseImprovement\%$ improvement
in outlet fidelity).
