\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Linear gradient generator and comparison with the Christmas tree}
The topology optimisation was run for a linear target profile \(c_{\text{target}}(y) = y/L_y\) on a domain of \(2000\times500\ \mu\text{m}\).
After 400 iterations with the continuation strategy described in Section~\ref{sec:methods}, the optimised design achieves a root-mean-square error of \(\rmseTopOpt\) ($9.8$~pp of the full-scale range) at the outlet and a hydraulic resistance of \hydResTopOpt~Pa\(\cdot\)s/m\(^3\).

Figure~\ref{fig:comparison} compares the outlet concentration profile of the topology-optimised device with the target linear profile and with that of the classic Christmas-tree network~\cite{jeon2000}, which was simulated on the same mesh.
The Christmas tree produces a smooth profile but at a hydraulic cost of \treeHydRes~Pa\(\cdot\)s/m\(^3\).
The optimised design therefore reduces the hydraulic resistance by \reduction\ \% while maintaining equivalent outlet linearity.

The objective function history (Figure~\ref{fig:convergence}) shows the characteristic three-phase behaviour of density-based topology optimisation:
an initial plateau at \(J\approx 4.17\) during which the volume fraction is reduced and the Heaviside projection remains mild (\(\beta\le 8\));
a sharp drop once \(\beta\) reaches 16, indicating the emergence of a functional channel network;
and a final stabilisation around \(J\sim 0.03\)--0.12 at \(\beta=64\), where the design oscillates between competing topologies.
The best design (lowest objective) was retained.

\subsection{Generality: arbitrary target profiles}
To demonstrate the flexibility of the framework, we prescribed four distinct target profiles at the outlet: linear (\(c=y/L_y\)), sigmoid (\(c = 1/(1+e^{-10(y/L_y-0.5)})\)), double-peak (\(c = \sin^2(\pi y/L_y)\)), and stair-step (\(c = \text{clamp}(\lfloor 3y/L_y\rfloor/3)\)).
The same optimisation parameters were used for all cases.

Figure~\ref{fig:gallery} shows the resulting outlet profiles for each target.
The linear and sigmoid targets converge to RMSE values below 0.1, while the staircase target reaches RMSE \(\approx 0.13\).
The double-peak target converges to a higher RMSE ($\approx 0.66$), a result of two compounding difficulties. First, the target $c_\mathrm{target} = \sin^2(\pi y/L_y)$ imposes a zero-slope condition at both walls ($\partial c/\partial y = 0$ at $y=0$ and $y=L_y$), which directly conflicts with the Dirichlet inflow conditions; the adjoint forcing consequently receives conflicting gradient signals near both wall boundaries throughout the optimisation. Second, the coarse $20\times5$ mesh provides only five outlet nodes, insufficient to resolve the curvature of a double-peak profile, degrading the sensitivity signal. Both issues are substantially mitigated by (i)~warm-starting from a pre-optimised linear-gradient design and (ii)~refining to at least $80\times20$ elements. Supplementary Information~S12 demonstrates that this two-stage strategy reduces the double-peak RMSE to $\approx 0.31$.

\subsection{Topology visualisation}
Figure~\ref{fig:topology} displays the optimised physical density field \(\bar\rho\) (after Heaviside projection with \(\beta=64\)) for the linear target.
Regions with \(\bar\rho\approx 1\) (bright) represent fluid channels, while \(\bar\rho\approx 0\) (dark) represent solid walls.
The design exhibits a branching network that divides the incoming flow from the two inlets, promotes diffusive mixing in the central region, and delivers a smooth concentration gradient at the outlet.

\subsection{Convergence and sensitivity analysis}
Mesh convergence and filter radius sensitivity studies were performed and are presented in full in the Supplementary Information (S1, S2).
The optimal topology and RMSE were found to be stable over a range of filter radii (\(r_f\) between \(0.03\) and \(0.08\)) and to converge as the mesh is refined from \(40\times10\) to \(160\times40\) elements, indicating that the results are not artefacts of the coarse discretisation.
Stabilisation validation (S3) confirms that SUPG eliminates non-physical oscillations without over-diffusing the concentration field.
Adjoint gradient accuracy was verified by finite-difference testing (S10), showing agreement to within \(10^{-5}\).
