We present \texttt{micrograd}, an open-source FEniCSx framework for
density-based topology optimisation of coupled Brinkman--convection-diffusion systems.
Four contributions advance the state of the art.

\textbf{(i) Continuous adjoint derivation.}
We derive the complete continuous adjoint for a multi-objective cost functional
coupling viscous dissipation and outlet concentration mismatch,
including the momentum--concentration coupling term $-\lambda\nabla c$
and the Dirichlet adjoint outlet condition.
This derivation has not previously been published for the
coupled Brinkman--convection-diffusion system.

\textbf{(ii) SUPG-stabilised adjoint at high P\'{e}clet numbers.}
At $\Pe\sim10^2$--$10^5$, both the forward and adjoint transport equations
require stabilisation.
We apply the same element-wise SUPG parameter to both equations,
ensuring numerical consistency; without stabilisation,
node-to-node oscillations of amplitude $\sim0.4$ corrupt the
sensitivity field and degrade optimisation.

\textbf{(iii) Modular continuation schedule.}
A five-component schedule (Helmholtz filtering, Heaviside $\beta$-sharpening
to $\beta=\betaMax$, $\alpha_{\max}$ ramping, hybrid OC/MMA,
sinusoidal initialisation) reduces the gray-zone fraction from
$\grayShortRun$ to $\grayFrac$ and RMSE from $\rmseShortRun$ to $\rmseTopOpt$
relative to a 600-iteration baseline.
Each component addresses a specific diagnosed failure mode
(Table~\ref{tab:continuation}).

\textbf{(iv) End-to-end reproducibility.}
All results are reproducible in approximately one hour on a standard laptop
from a single Docker command:
{\small\texttt{docker run \ldots dolfinx/dolfinx:v0.7.3 bash run\_all.sh}}.
The pipeline is verified by continuous integration on every commit
and archived with a Zenodo DOI~\cite{micrograd2025zenodo}.

As a benchmark, the framework achieves outlet RMSE~$=\rmseTopOpt$
for a linear concentration gradient,
with framework generality demonstrated on sigmoid (RMSE~$=\rmseSigmoid$)
and Gaussian peak (RMSE~$=\rmseGaussian$) targets.
\texttt{micrograd} is deliberately a surrogate tool for rapid inverse design
in porous media: it identifies high-performance continuous-permeability concepts
that inform subsequent binary design or experimental iteration.
