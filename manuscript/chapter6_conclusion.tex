\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented \texttt{micrograd}, an open-source FEniCSx framework
for density-based topology optimisation of coupled
Brinkman--convection-diffusion systems.
The paper makes the following methodological contributions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A complete continuous adjoint derivation for the coupled
          Brinkman--convection-diffusion system, with the coupling
          term $-\lambda\nabla c$ in the momentum adjoint and a
          Dirichlet outlet condition for the concentration adjoint
          derived from the misfit functional.

    \item Consistent SUPG stabilisation applied to both the forward
          and adjoint transport equations, using the same element-wise
          stabilisation parameter, for numerical robustness at high
          P\'{e}clet numbers.

    \item A hybrid OC/MMA optimisation strategy with five nested
          continuation strategies ($\alpha_{\max}$ ramping,
          $\beta$-sharpening to $\beta=128$, Helmholtz filtering,
          volume targeting, sinusoidal initialisation) that reduces
          the gray-zone fraction from $0.443$ at $\beta=16$ to
          $\grayFrac=0.074$ at $\beta=128$.

    \item Gradient accuracy verified by finite-difference Taylor
          remainder test (slope $\approx 2.0$) and direction test
          (Pearson correlation $\geq 0.80$), together confirming
          consistent adjoint implementation.

    \item Full reproducibility via Docker, continuous integration,
          and Zenodo archival; all results are recoverable from
          a single command.
\end{itemize}

As a benchmark application, the framework is demonstrated on microfluidic
concentration gradient generation.
On an $80\times20$ mesh, the Brinkman surrogate optimum achieves
RMSE~$=\rmseTopOpt=0.058$ for a linear target profile, with gray-zone
fraction $\grayFrac=0.074$.
The surrogate reduces hydraulic resistance by $\reduction\%$ relative
to the best Christmas-tree configuration.
Hard-thresholding to a thresholded-permeability design increases RMSE to $0.359$,
comparable to the best parametric baseline ($0.329$);
the surrogate advantage is a theoretical bound on what is achievable
with a continuously graded porous medium.

Volume-fraction sweep ($V^*\in\{0.3,0.5,0.7\}$) and additional target
profiles (sigmoid, Gaussian peak) confirm that the framework generalises
without modification; achievable RMSE depends on the target's
compatibility with diffusive mixing at the operating P\'{e}clet number.

\paragraph{Future work.}
The principal open directions are:
(i)~binary realisation via robust projection~\cite{wang2011} or
iterative thresholding;
(ii)~a discrete adjoint for exact gradient magnitude;
(iii)~three-dimensional extension and experimental fabrication;
(iv)~adaptive mesh refinement targeting the diffusion sublayer
($\delta\approx 4\,\mu$m).

The framework is designed to be general: any differentiable functional
of velocity and concentration fields can be incorporated by modifying
the objective and adjoint boundary conditions in the UFL description.
This generality, combined with a computational cost of under thirty
minutes on a standard laptop and full end-to-end reproducibility,
makes \texttt{micrograd} a practical foundation for surrogate-based
computational microfluidic optimisation and a reference implementation
for coupled flow-transport adjoint methods in FEniCSx.
